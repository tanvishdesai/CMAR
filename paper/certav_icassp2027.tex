\begin{abstract}
Audio-visual deepfake detectors increasingly rely on cross-modal cues, yet most robustness evaluations remain empirical and do not provide per-sample guarantees. This paper introduces CertAV, a feature-space certification pipeline for audio-visual deepfake detection. CertAV freezes DINOv2 visual features and Whisper audio features, fuses them with a temporal cross-modal detector, and applies Gaussian randomized smoothing over the joint feature space. Under this threat model, the smoothed classifier returns a prediction, an abstention decision, and an $\ell_2$ certified radius with 99.9\% confidence. On FakeAVCeleb, across five random seeds, CertAV reaches $92.5\pm0.6$\% clean accuracy at $\sigma=1.0$, with mean certified radius $2.215\pm0.032$, $88.0$\% certified accuracy at radius 1.0, $84.9$\% at radius 1.5, and less than 1\% abstention. A no-noise baseline certifies nearly as well, while feature-space PGD adversarial training substantially lowers validation AUC, suggesting that the certifiability comes primarily from the geometry of frozen multimodal representations. Transfer, degradation, feature-displacement, and manifold analyses support the same conclusion while clarifying the scope of the certificate.
\end{abstract}

audio-visual deepfake detection, certified robustness, randomized smoothing, multimodal robustness, media forensics

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Audio-visual deepfake detection has moved from single-stream artifact recognition toward cross-modal reasoning: whether a face, voice, expression, and temporal speech pattern belong to the same event. This shift is natural, since modern forgeries can alter either modality or both. It also creates a robustness problem. A detector may be accurate under clean evaluation, yet remain vulnerable to small adversarial feature shifts, compression artifacts, or cross-dataset changes. For forensic use, a calibrated detector is useful; a detector with a per-sample robustness certificate is stronger.

Randomized smoothing offers such certificates by converting a base classifier into a smoothed classifier whose prediction is provably unchanged within an $\ell_2$ ball around the input representation \cite{cohen2019certified}. Its most common use has been image-space classification, where the certified radius is measured directly in pixels. Audio-visual deepfake detection is less straightforward: videos are long, audio is continuous, and the detector often relies on frozen self-supervised encoders. CertAV therefore certifies the detector in the feature space induced by frozen DINOv2 \cite{oquab2024dinov2} and Whisper \cite{radford2023whisper} encoders. This is a deliberately scoped guarantee: it does not certify arbitrary waveform or pixel perturbations, but it gives a precise robustness statement for the representation space actually consumed by the detector.

The main result is unexpectedly simple. Feature-space smoothing gives large certified radii with little abstention, and the strongest explanation is not that noise-augmented training alone makes the detector robust. Instead, the frozen multimodal representation appears to be low-dimensional relative to its ambient feature space, allowing isotropic Gaussian smoothing to move samples substantially without crossing the decision boundary.

This paper makes four contributions. First, it defines CertAV, a feature-space randomized smoothing pipeline for audio-visual deepfake detection under a joint audio-visual $\ell_2$ threat model. Second, it reports a five-seed certification study on FakeAVCeleb \cite{khalid2021fakeavceleb}, reaching $88.0$\% certified accuracy at radius 1.0 and $84.9$\% at radius 1.5. Third, it analyzes negative and diagnostic results: unimodal certificates can be numerically competitive but protect narrower threat models, a no-noise baseline remains certifiable, and feature-space PGD adversarial training trades away validation AUC. Fourth, it packages the evaluation logic as \texttt{av-robustbench}, a benchmark scaffold for attacks, smoothing, degradations, transfer tests, and robustness cards.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\textbf{Audio-visual deepfake detection.}
Audio-visual detectors exploit inconsistencies that may not be visible in either modality alone. Earlier work studied affective disagreement \cite{mittal2020emotions} and audio-visual dissonance \cite{chugh2020not}. Later methods use self-supervised anomaly detection \cite{feng2023self}, joint audio-visual learning \cite{yang2023avoid}, and local temporal inconsistency modeling \cite{astrid2025local}. Datasets such as FakeAVCeleb \cite{khalid2021fakeavceleb} and LAV-DF \cite{cai2022lavdf} provide multimodal evaluation settings with manipulated visual and/or audio streams. These works improve detection accuracy and localization, but their robustness claims are usually empirical.

\textbf{Certified robustness.}
Randomized smoothing certifies an $\ell_2$ ball around an input by estimating the probability that a Gaussian-perturbed base classifier predicts the top class \cite{cohen2019certified}. Differential-privacy-based certification \cite{lecuyer2019certified}, adversarially trained smoothed classifiers \cite{salman2019provably}, and generalized smoothing distributions \cite{yang2020randomized} further show that randomized certification can scale when exact verification is impractical. CertAV follows the same certification principle, but applies it to frozen audio-visual feature representations rather than to raw images.

\textbf{Robustness benchmarks.}
RobustBench standardized adversarial robustness reporting for image classifiers \cite{croce2021robustbench}. Audio-visual deepfake detection lacks a similarly compact benchmark interface for attacks, certificates, degradations, and transfer reporting. The \texttt{av-robustbench} component of this project follows the RobustBench philosophy, but adapts the threat models and report format to multimodal forensic detectors.

\subsection{Feature representation and detector}
\label{ssec:detector}

Given an audio-video clip $\x$, CertAV extracts visual and audio feature sequences
\begin{equation}
  Z_v = E_v(\x_v), \qquad Z_a = E_a(\x_a),
\end{equation}
where $E_v$ is a frozen DINOv2-Small encoder and $E_a$ is a frozen Whisper-tiny encoder. The visual branch samples 16 frames and returns 384-dimensional frame embeddings. The audio branch processes 16 kHz audio up to 10 seconds and returns 384-dimensional temporal embeddings. Features are cached and used as the input domain for training, attacks, and certification.

The base detector $f_\theta$ first maps each modality to a 256-dimensional temporal sequence. The visual branch uses a transformer temporal aggregator with learned positional embeddings; the audio branch uses temporal pooling and normalization. A two-layer cross-modal consistency module performs bidirectional attention: visual tokens attend to audio tokens and audio tokens attend to visual tokens. The fused 256-dimensional sequence is passed through a binary classifier, and segment logits are averaged before the final real/fake decision.

\subsection{Threat model}
\label{ssec:threat}

Let $\z=(Z_v,Z_a)$ denote the concatenated frozen representation. CertAV certifies robustness to joint feature perturbations $\del=(\delta_v,\delta_a)$ satisfying
\begin{equation}
  \|\del\|_2 \le r.
\end{equation}
This threat model is narrower than raw waveform or raw pixel certification, but it is exact for attacks on the representation consumed by the detector. We also evaluate visual-only and audio-only smoothing, where $\delta$ is restricted to one modality. Those certificates can have larger numerical radii because they protect a lower-dimensional perturbation set; they are not a replacement for the joint threat model.

\subsection{Noise-augmented training}
\label{ssec:training}

During noise-augmented training, Gaussian noise is injected into the selected feature channels:
\begin{equation}
  \tilde{\z} = \z + \eps,\qquad \eps \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2 I).
\end{equation}
The model is trained with binary cross-entropy,
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}(\theta) =
  -y\log f_\theta(\tilde{\z})-(1-y)\log(1-f_\theta(\tilde{\z})).
\end{equation}
We train joint, visual-only, and audio-only variants at $\sigma=0.12$, 0.25, 0.50, and 1.00 over five seeds. A no-noise model and a feature-space PGD adversarially trained model are included as baselines.

\subsection{Certification}
\label{ssec:certification}

The smoothed classifier is
\begin{equation}
  g(\z)=\arg\max_c \Pr_{\eps\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2 I)}
  \left(f_\theta(\z+\eps)=c\right).
\end{equation}
Certification follows the standard two-stage Monte Carlo procedure \cite{cohen2019certified}. First, $n_0=100$ samples select the candidate class $A$. Then $n=1000$ samples estimate its probability. A one-sided Clopper-Pearson lower confidence bound $\punder$ is computed with $\alpha=0.001$. If $\punder\le0.5$, the classifier abstains. Otherwise, the certified radius is
\begin{equation}
  \Rcert = \sigma \Phi^{-1}(\punder),
\end{equation}
where $\Phi^{-1}$ is the inverse standard normal CDF. Certified accuracy at radius $r$ is the fraction of test examples that are correct, non-abstained, and satisfy $\Rcert\ge r$.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Setup}

FakeAVCeleb is split deterministically into 3850 training clips, 825 validation clips, and 825 test clips, with a 1:10 real/fake ratio in each split. All reported main results average five seeds: 42, 69, 420, 2026, and 2804. Models are trained with AdamW, learning rate $5\cdot10^{-4}$, weight decay 0.01, batch size 8, gradient accumulation 4, cosine decay, 3 warmup epochs, gradient clipping at 1.0, and early stopping. Validation predictions average 10 noisy samples. Cross-dataset transfer is evaluated on 500 LAV-DF samples. Unless stated otherwise, certification uses $n_0=100$, $n=1000$, and $\alpha=0.001$.

The ablation in Table \ref{tab:baselines} is central to the interpretation of the project. Noise-augmented training improves validation separation and gives the strongest overall balance, but the no-noise baseline certifies nearly as well. Conversely, feature-space PGD adversarial training produces large certified radii at the cost of much lower validation AUC and EER. The practical conclusion is that CertAV should not be sold as a simple adversarial-training recipe. Its main value is to expose and certify a favorable geometry already present in frozen audio-visual representations.

Cross-dataset transfer is harder than in-domain certification. On LAV-DF, the $\sigma=1.0$ model reaches 74.6\% accuracy and 61.4\% certified accuracy at radius 1.0. This is a useful transfer signal, but it also shows that dataset shift remains a real limitation. Common media degradations are less damaging: JPEG, H.264, and social-media-style degradation preserve about 90\% certified accuracy at radius 0.50. Finally, feature-displacement validation perturbs cached features with controlled $\ell_2$ displacement. The certified prediction holds for 96.0\% of samples at displacement 0.25 and 91.0\% at displacement 1.00. This supports the feature-space certificate, while remaining distinct from a formal raw-input guarantee.

\subsection{Why are the radii large?}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/manifold_smoothing.png}
\caption{The frozen multimodal representation has low effective dimension, which helps explain why large Gaussian neighborhoods can preserve predictions.}
\label{fig:manifold}
\vspace{-0.2cm}
\end{figure}

The representation analysis gives a compact explanation. Visual features require 73 of 384 principal components for 90\% variance and 113 for 95\%. Audio features require only 13 and 36 components. The concatenated joint representation requires 75 of 768 components for 90\% variance and 116 for 95\%. At $\sigma=1.0$, the prediction flip rate under smoothing is 1.0\%. Thus, the data occupy a relatively small subspace of the ambient representation, and the classifier boundary appears far from most samples along the directions explored by Gaussian noise.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

CertAV certifies frozen feature representations, not raw pixels or waveforms. This is the right level for the current detector and cached-feature evaluation pipeline, but it is not an end-to-end physical-world guarantee. The cross-dataset LAV-DF result is promising but not yet strong enough to claim universal generalization across manipulation families. The study also uses one backbone pair, DINOv2 and Whisper; future work should test whether the same low-dimensional geometry appears in other visual, speech, and audio-visual foundation encoders. Finally, randomized smoothing is computationally expensive because each certified example requires many noisy classifier evaluations.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

CertAV shows that certified robustness is feasible for audio-visual deepfake detection when the guarantee is placed at the level of frozen multimodal features. Across five seeds on FakeAVCeleb, feature-space randomized smoothing gives high certified accuracy, low abstention, and large per-sample radii. The strongest lesson is not that a particular training trick solves robust deepfake detection, but that modern frozen audio and visual representations can have a geometry that is unusually friendly to certification. That makes feature-space certificates a practical bridge between empirical forensic detectors and the stronger guarantees needed for high-stakes media verification.
